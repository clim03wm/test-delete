Economy Philosophy:Cause of Great Depression

The Great Depression was one of the worst economic collapses in history, and people have debated its causes for nearly a century. The Hegemonic Stability Theory offers the clearest explanation. The main reason for the Great Depression was that the U.S. failed to take on its role as the global leader, over-liberalized its market, and passed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act. While liberalism, later neoliberalism, played a part in the crisis, it survived because its focus on efficiency remained appealing during hard times.

America’s refusal to step into its role as the new global hegemon and its reckless economic choices, such as extreme market liberalization and the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, were one of the main reasons for the Great Depression. The Hegemonic Stability Theory explains that the world economy functions best when a dominant power provides leadership, stability, and public goods like open markets and financial liquidity. As Lairson and Skidmore describe, a hegemon maintains order by “promoting and protecting a liberal international economy” through its control of trade, capital flows, and institutions (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). When that leadership disappears, smaller nations neither have the means nor the motivation to coordinate policy, and the system begins to fragment. In short, global prosperity depends on a dominant power’s willingness to act for the common good, even at its own cost.

After World War I, the United States had both the wealth and the industrial might to stabilize the global economy, but refused to do so. It declined to join the League of Nations, signaling a lack of commitment to international cooperation and engagement. Instead of helping Europe recover, the U.S. demanded full repayment of war debts, draining fragile economies already struggling to rebuild (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). At the same time, it hoarded gold reserves rather than extending credit or liquidity to stabilize global markets (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). This absence of leadership left the postwar system without direction, allowing instability to grow until it finally broke.

The same blindness appeared in America’s domestic policy. The government held firm to its belief that markets would self-correct, even as speculation spiraled out of control. Financial institutions were left to gamble freely, and when the debt bubble burst, the consequences spread far beyond U.S. borders. Marx had predicted this kind of collapse, warning that capitalism’s reliance on “competitive markets, commodity production, and financial speculation” would eventually produce crises that no one could control (Panitch 2009). His warning became reality when the Federal Reserve tightened credit to restrain speculation, setting off a chain reaction that turned a domestic downturn into a worldwide catastrophe (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). As Rodrik later observed, “markets and states are complements,” meaning that neither can sustain itself without the other (Rodrik 2012). The U.S., trusting too much in the market and too little in its own responsibility, failed on both fronts. Without a hegemon to stabilize and guide the global economy, once the role of Great Britain, now weakened and immobilized after World War I, worsened the crash.

Tariff policy made this worse. The Fordney-McCumber Tariff of 1922 “further undermined the ability of Europeans to earn the foreign exchange needed to pay for debts,” forcing nations into a cycle of deflation and dependence on American loans (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). The Smoot-Hawley Tariff of 1930 “substantially increased U.S. protectionism and triggered a series of retaliatory tariff increases around the world,” collapsing global trade when recovery depended on openness (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). At the time, the U.S. was both the industrial heartland of the world. Many nations relied on access to American markets to sell goods, rebuild their industries, and earn the dollars needed to service debts. Yet Washington turned inward, convinced that its domestic prosperity did not depend on the global system it helped create. This misjudgment choked trade, provoked retaliation, and deepened the Depression. As Rodrik notes, tariffs impose “clear transaction costs on international exchanges,” distorting trade and blocking the flow of goods and capital that global economies depend on (Rodrik 2012). By isolating itself, America not only hurt its trading partners, it weakened the very foundations of its own success.

Despite these failures, liberalism, and later neoliberalism, returned to dominance. The collapse of the 1930s had discredited unregulated capitalism, and in the years that followed, most nations embraced Keynesian economics. Governments intervened in markets, built social safety nets, and worked to maintain stability. As the Commanding Heights documentary explains, “a crisis is a catalyst for a change in economics”. This Keynesian model worked for decades, especially during the postwar boom. Yet by the 1970s, the global economy was slowing. Inflation rose, unemployment spread, and confidence in state-led economic management began to fade. This stagnation, “stagflation”, became another crisis that prompted a shift in ideology. As Panitch explains, the turmoil of the 1970s “opened the way for a reassertion of capitalist logic,” paving the way for market-oriented reforms aimed at restoring private confidence and reducing government control (Panitch 2009). In the United States, Ronald Reagan used this frustration to promote deregulation, lower taxes, and smaller government, promising renewed growth through market freedom. His message resonated with a public weary of stagnation and with corporations eager to escape postwar constraints. Monbiot describes this shift as the victory of an ideology that “redefined citizens as consumers” and recast social cooperation as competition, treating inequality as a sign of efficiency rather than failure (Monbiot 2016). By the 1980s, neoliberalism had reshaped not just U.S. policy but much of the Western world. It sold the idea of growth through freedom, responsibility, and open markets, even though those same principles had helped cause the Great Depression fifty years earlier. As Panitch reminds, neoliberalism’s revival was not only about economics but about restoring class power, the idea that markets, not democracy, should determine the direction of society (Panitch 2009). Once again, the crisis opened the door for liberalism’s return, showing how easily old ideologies reappear, dressed as new solutions to the very problems they helped create.

The Great Depression had many causes, but the Hegemonic Stability Theory captures its core better than any other explanation. America’s unwillingness to assume the responsibilities of a global leader left the world without a stabilizing force. With no state capable of guiding trade, credit, or recovery, the system fell apart. At the same time, America’s blind faith in a liberal market, its refusal to strengthen government protection, and its tariff policies all deepened the collapse. The Great Depression became the darkest reflection of what happens when free markets are left to themselves. Yet, despite its failures, liberalism, and later neoliberalism, never disappeared. People return to it in times of stagnation, drawn by its promise of efficiency and revival. Just as the Great Depression made people turn to Keynesian economics, the economic stagnation of the 1970s became another turning point, pushing society back toward neoliberalism. When economies slow down and faith in government solutions fades, liberal markets re-emerge as the supposed cure. The pattern repeats: crisis leads to doubt, and doubt becomes the catalyst for ideological change. Though liberalism failed in the 1930s, each new period of stagnation brings it back, repackaged, rationalized, and ready to try again.
