Double Edged Sword:Sweatshop

Globalization, with free trade with multinational corporations, has grown at an unprecedented pace. These forces brought prosperity to large corporations and, to a degree, boosted developing nations’ economies. Yet they also harmed industrial workers and the manufacturing industry in advanced nations. As factories moved overseas and jobs disappeared, frustration grew. This eventually fueled the rise of populism across the developed world.

Sweatshops have given a massive economic and industrial boost to many developing countries. Although many see sweatshops as symbols of exploitation, that view oversimplifies their role in the economies of developing nations. Compared to Western standards, the conditions are harsh, with long hours, low wages, and limited safety, but for many workers, these jobs represent the best available option. In countries where alternatives are unstable or informal, such as subsistence farming or street vending, factory employment provides a more consistent income and a chance at upward mobility. Bangladesh’s garment industry, for example, began under such conditions and has since become a cornerstone of its national economy. Moreover, Bangladesh’s GDP has risen about 176% since 2000 (World Bank 2023) and has outperformed its neighbors in life expectancy, infant mortality, and education (Dreze & Sen 2012). Interviewed, many of the workers within the sweatshops were more than willing to work in such harsh conditions due to its pay (CBS 2012). Although the entirety of Bangladesh’s economic growth cannot be credited to sweatshops, it is clear that they are helping developing countries like Bangladesh to improve their economic growth.

Sweatshops have also played a central role in China’s rapid rise from a poor agrarian society to the world’s second-largest economy. For millions of rural migrants, factory jobs in coastal cities offered something their villages could not: stable wages, housing, and access to modern infrastructure. Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, China became the world’s manufacturing hub, producing low-cost goods that fueled export growth and attracted massive foreign investment. This industrial base later allowed China to climb the value chain, moving from textiles and toys to electronics and advanced manufacturing. The results are undeniable: China’s GDP grew more than 9% annually for three decades, and over 800 million people were lifted out of poverty (World Bank 2020).

China’s rise as a manufacturing powerhouse transformed global trade and allowed Western corporations to profit enormously from low-cost production. Companies like Walmart were among the first to recognize the advantages of outsourcing to Asia, shifting their supply chains from domestic producers to Chinese and other developing-country factories. This move let them source goods at a fraction of Western costs while maintaining price markups of around 20% (FRONTLINE PBS 2004). As competition intensified, other major retailers followed suit, driving a global race to secure the cheapest labor and materials. This strategy enabled corporations to keep consumer prices low while preserving profit margins, a system that fueled corporate growth even as it deepened dependence on low-wage manufacturing abroad.

However, the shift of production to Asian markets came at a steep cost for Western economies, particularly in manufacturing. As corporations moved their supply chains overseas, industrial bases in the United States and other developed nations began to erode, resulting in widespread job losses. A clear example of this decline is the collapse of the domestic high-tech and television industries in the U.S. Faced with the low production costs of Asian competitors, American and European manufacturers struggled to stay afloat. The Thomson plant in Circleville, Ohio, a French-owned television producer, fell into steady decline after 2002 as Chinese firms such as APEX and TCL dominated the market. Within a few years, American manufacturers had lost nearly one-third of the high-end television market, a loss valued at around $350 million, forcing many plants, including Thomson’s, to shut down entirely (FRONTLINE PBS 2004). Free trade and multinational corporate expansion may have lowered consumer prices, but they also hollowed out industrial communities across America, leaving behind unemployment and economic decay.

The decline of U.S. steel production stands as another great example of how free trade and globalization reshaped the American economy, a devastating blow to the working class. For much of the 20th century, states like Pennsylvania and Ohio were the industrial center of the nation, home to cities such as Pittsburgh, Youngstown, and Cleveland, where steel mills provided stable, middle-class jobs. These plants were a backbone to many communities, as many of the families have worked for generations within those steel plants (Froman 2025). However, as global trade liberalized in the 1970s and 1980s, new international competition, particularly from Japan, South Korea, and later China, flooded the market with cheaper steel. American producers, bound by higher labor costs, environmental standards, and pension obligations, could not compete. By 1980, foreign steel made up nearly one-fifth of the U.S. market, and by 2000, China had become the world’s dominant steel producer (Lairson & Skidmore 2017). Between 1979 and 1986, the U.S. steel industry lost over 200,000 jobs, more than half of its total workforce (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics 1987). In Pennsylvania alone, employment in steel manufacturing fell by over 80%, leading some to a collapse of entire local economies dependent on mill wages. Towns like Aliquippa, Homestead, and Youngstown became symbols of deindustrialization, a clearer loss from free trade and MNC activities. Globalization “intensified the asymmetries between developed and developing economies,” leaving regions like the Rust Belt vulnerable to industrial decline (Lairson & Skidmore 2017).

For many, the promise of reviving lost manufacturing jobs, especially in industries like steel and aluminum, is deeply appealing. Populist leaders have capitalized on this sentiment, calling for tariffs and stronger market controls to shield domestic industries from foreign competition. This rhetoric echoes earlier efforts, such as the Bush administration’s consideration of steel tariffs, which were framed as measures to protect American workers. In communities where entire local economies collapsed after factories closed, the idea of those jobs returning carries powerful emotional and political weight. Politicians have used that hope effectively, particularly in swing states like Pennsylvania and Ohio, where economic decline has been most visible. For example, under the Trump administration, a 25% tariff on steel was introduced to protect domestic producers. While the policy may have created roughly 1,000 new jobs in the steel sector, it simultaneously raised costs for downstream industries, leading to an estimated loss of 75,000 manufacturing jobs that relied on steel and aluminum (Froman 2025). This outcome highlights the tension at the core of modern populism, it resonates with voters who feel abandoned by globalization but often delivers mixed or counterproductive results once implemented.

Although free trade and multinational corporate activity have greatly benefited developing nations such as China and Bangladesh, and boosted the profit margins of major retailers like Walmart, they have also revealed a significant downside: the large-scale loss of manufacturing jobs in industries such as steel, aluminum, and electronics across states like Pennsylvania and Ohio. Many towns built around these factories saw their entire local economies collapse once production moved overseas. In the wake of this decline, populism has gained momentum, fueled by promises to restore lost manufacturing jobs through tariffs and protectionist policies. Yet, despite its growing appeal, the effectiveness of this approach remains deeply uncertain.
